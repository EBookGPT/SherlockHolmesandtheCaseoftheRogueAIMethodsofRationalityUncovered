In Sherlock Holmes' journey to solve the mystery of the rogue AI, he employed a code that allowed him to analyze evidence and update his hypotheses. Now, let us further elaborate on the workings of this remarkable algorithm.

Code Breakdown

The code used by Sherlock Holmes embodies Bayesian reasoning through the creation of a function that updates the probability of a hypothesis based on new evidence. This forms the crux of Bayesian inference, where probability estimates are dynamically adjusted upon the revelation of additional data.

def update_probability(hypothesis, evidence):
    prior_probability = hypothesis.default_probability()
    likelihood = evidence.probability_given(hypothesis)
    marginal_likelihood = sum(e.probability_given(h) for h in hypothesis.hypothesis_space())
    return (likelihood * prior_probability) / marginal_likelihood

Function Components

The function update_probability accepts two arguments: a hypothesis (representing one possible explanation for the observed facts) and a piece of evidence (an element that either supports or undermines a given hypothesis). The function aims to calculate the revised probability of the hypothesis based on this new piece of evidence.

Within the function, the following calculations are performed:

  1. prior_probability is determined by calling the default_probability() method on the hypothesis, which represents the initial probability estimate based on existing knowledge, before obtaining the new evidence.

  2. likelihood is calculated by calling the probability_given() method on the evidence, focusing on the probability of the evidence being observed, considering the hypothesis to be true.

  3. marginal_likelihood is the likelihood of the evidence, averaged across all possible hypotheses, accounting for their individual probabilities. This step transforms the sum into a weighted average and is crucial in determining how much influence the additional evidence has on a hypothesis.

  4. The function returns an updated probability, calculated by multiplying the prior_probability and the likelihood, then dividing the result by the marginal_likelihood. This new probability, called the posterior probability, forms the rationale's basis for either accepting or rejecting the hypothesis based on the evidence presented.
