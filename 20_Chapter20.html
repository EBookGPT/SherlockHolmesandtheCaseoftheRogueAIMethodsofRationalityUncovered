Today, Sherlock Holmes and Alan Turing demonstrated the power of Bayesian inference in solving challenging mysteries. They started with a given hypothesis, which in this case was the assumption that the encoded message was a Caesar cipher with an unknown shift value. Each possible shift value became a hypothesis H[i], with the prior probabilities P(H[i]) evenly distributed as they had no reason to prefer any specific shift value.

# Probabilistic model:
# hypotheses H[i]: Caesar cipher shift value i
# priors P(H[i]) = 1/26, for i in range(0, 26)

As new evidence—an encoded message—was presented, they utilized Bayesian inference to update their beliefs. The likelihood function, P(E|H[i]), determined how probable the evidence was under each hypothesis, revealing the potential shift values.

# Likelihood function:
# P(E|H[i]) = likelihood of the encoded message E, considering H[i]

Using Bayes' rule, they calculated the posterior probability of each hypothesis, that is, the updated probability based on the available evidence: P(H[i]|E) = P(E|H[i]) * P(H[i])/P(E). To simplify calculations, they normalized the posterior probabilities, ensuring their sum equaled one. After updating their beliefs, the highest posterior probability revealed the true Caesar cipher shift value, allowing them to decode the message.
